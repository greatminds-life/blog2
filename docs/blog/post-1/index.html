Private schools may cost the same as public schools if well planned

By Joe Jo / April 26 2020 - 5 min read

Colleges are insanely expensive in US

Preparing funds to pay college tuition to send kids to colleges is a serious topic in US since cost of attendance is very expensive. You can grasp the idea how much it will cost from the table below. These numbers are based on 2020-2021 academic year which means you would need x4 times of fund to completely cover 4 years of education. This is only for undergraduate schools. On the average, it may require at least $30K a year up to $75K a year. If you have pile of cash to cover this, good for you. If not, question comes up, how to prepare that?

Common misunderstandings

  1. my kids are too young, it’s too early to save
    Considering inflation over time, it will cost more when younger kids go to college.
  2. our income is too high, there is no way we can get financial aid
    Income levels are all relative, and what if one parent loses job when applying?
  3. our income is too log, we never have to pay
    No guarantee to get good financial aid.
  4. public schools are much cheaper, forget about private
    If well planned, you may pay the same amount.

So the take-away here is that it is better to start preparing sooner, regardless of your income level.

How ‘NEEDS’ are calculated?

For need-based grant, NEED is calculated as in the following equation.

NEED = COA - EFC
where
COA : Cost Of Attendance - determined by school
EFC : Expected Family Contribution - money from my pocket

Apparently, when your needs become big, you will get higher chance of getting grant with more amount. Then question is how to maximize the needs? As you can see from the equation, we can only have control over EFC since COA is fixed value. If we minimize our EFC, our NEEDS will be maximized given COA.

EFC is determined by the following four factors:

  1. parent income
  2. parent asset
  3. student income
  4. student asset

How to lower EFC?

This is about to minimize the 4 factors contributing EFC - parent income, parent asset, student income and student asset.

minimizing parent income

As an employee, there is not much room to lower parent income since it is all reported and tracked. One way to get around is to start or get involved in a business apart from day time job so that you can file expenses from the business which will lower your income level.

minimizing parent asset

Asset types which will be included in calculating EFC are as follows:

529
UGMA/UTMA
Stock/Mutual fund/ETF
Savings/CD
Rental property
Trust fund
Resident home (public schools may exclude)

Assets excluded from EFC:

Qualified retirement fund : 401k, IRA, Roth,…
Qualified annuity
IGIP(7702) : IUL(Indexed Universal Life insurance), …

Based on this, plan should be focusing reallocating assets from includable to excludable.
If you’re paying mortgage, re-financing would lower the equity value of the house thus decrease EFC.
529, student expense fund, would require special attention cause some school would immediately disqualify the aid application if you were already putting money in 529. Life insurance products, such as IUL(Indexed Universal Life Insurance), could be used to lower includable assets. You could also get tax benefits as well.

minimizing student income

If student has taxable income, like working as part timer at McDonalds, it will also be included in EFC, not a great idea considering the aid amount could have been granted otherwise.

minimizing student asset

Having savings or brokerage account under kids name, especially with good amount of money in them, would be included as student asset. Also, if you have house(s) under your kids name, that will be included as well so not recommended.
